Barabar Caves

The Barabar Caves, dating back to the Maurya period, are the oldest surviving rock-cut caves in India. Located in Jehanabad district, they were excavated during Emperor Ashoka’s reign and dedicated mainly to the Ajivika sect. Famous for their polished interiors and precise craftsmanship, these caves also carry inscriptions of Ashoka and his grandson Dasaratha. They remain an outstanding example of early Indian rock architecture and religious heritage.

History & Significance

The Barabar Caves, carved into granite hills, were created in the 3rd century BCE during Emperor Ashoka’s reign and later under his grandson, Dasaratha. They are the earliest examples of rock-cut architecture in India, with remarkable interior polish that creates an echo effect. Dedicated to the Ajivika sect, the caves also feature Buddhist and Hindu associations over time. Four main caves—Karan Chaupar, Lomas Rishi, Sudama, and Vishvakarma—showcase intricate craftsmanship and inscriptions. Their architectural style influenced later cave traditions like Ajanta and Ellora. The site remains a cultural and religious milestone reflecting India’s ancient architectural brilliance and spiritual legacy.

Transport

The Barabar Caves are located about 25 km north of Gaya in Bihar. Gaya Junction railway station is the nearest rail hub, providing strong connectivity to Patna, Delhi, and Kolkata. The closest airport is Gaya International Airport, around 30 km away, with Patna Airport at about 100 km for broader air travel options. The caves are accessible by taxis, buses, and private vehicles from Gaya or Jehanabad. The approach road winds through rural landscapes, leading to the granite hills that house the caves. Local facilities are limited, so visitors are advised to plan accordingly when traveling to this historic site.
