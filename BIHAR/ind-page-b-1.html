Mahabodhi Temple

The Mahabodhi Temple in Bodh Gaya is Buddhism’s most revered shrine, marking where Siddhartha Gautama attained enlightenment beneath the Bodhi tree. Originally established by Emperor Ashoka in the 3rd century BCE and rebuilt in later centuries, the brick temple complex features a soaring central spire, stupas, monasteries and the living Bodhi tree descendant. A UNESCO World Heritage Site, it draws pilgrims, monks, and visitors worldwide for meditation and devotion.

History & Significance

The Mahabodhi Temple complex stands at the exact site of the Bodhi tree, where the Buddha attained enlightenment around the 6th–5th century BCE. Emperor Ashoka visited and established a shrine in the 3rd century BCE, initiating a long tradition of devotion. The principal brick temple, largely formed in later centuries and notably during the Gupta era, features the Vajrasana (diamond throne), intricate reliefs, and numerous votive stupas. Over time the complex has been restored and supported by international Buddhist communities, remaining a global hub for pilgrimage, scholarship, and meditation.

Transport

Bodh Gaya is well connected: Gaya International Airport lies about 12 km away with domestic and some international flights; Gaya Junction railway station is roughly 16 km from the site and offers regular train connections. From Patna (state capital) the road distance is about 110 km, typically a three-hour drive by car or bus. Regular taxis, buses, and auto-rickshaws operate between Gaya and Bodh Gaya. Local infrastructure includes hotels, monasteries offering stays, and clear signposting near the temple to assist pilgrims and tourists.
