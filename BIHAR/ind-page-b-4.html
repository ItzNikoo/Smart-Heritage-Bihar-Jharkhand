Vikramshila University Ruins

Vikramshila University, founded in the 8th century by King Dharmapala of the Pala dynasty, was a major Buddhist learning center in eastern India. Known for Tantric Buddhism studies, it attracted scholars from across Asia. The sprawling red-brick ruins near Bhagalpur showcase monasteries, stupas, and a grand central hall. Although destroyed in the 12th century, the site remains a significant archaeological and cultural landmark, reflecting Bihar’s scholarly and spiritual heritage.

History & Significance

Established in the 8th century by King Dharmapala, Vikramshila University rose to prominence alongside Nalanda as a distinguished center of Buddhist learning. It specialized in Vajrayana or Tantric Buddhism, attracting monks, scholars, and translators from Tibet, China, and beyond. The university complex once housed more than 100 teachers and thousands of students. Excavations reveal a vast campus with a cruciform monastery, lecture halls, stupas, and residential quarters. Despite its destruction during invasions in the 12th century, the ruins still testify to Bihar’s intellectual and cultural influence in medieval Asia, making it a site of great historical and spiritual significance.

Transport

The ruins of Vikramshila are located near Antichak village in Bhagalpur district, about 50 km from Bhagalpur city. The nearest railway station is Kahalgaon, approximately 11 km away, offering regional connectivity. Bhagalpur Junction provides broader rail access to major cities like Patna and Kolkata. The closest airport is Patna Airport, about 270 km away, with road connections via national highways. Visitors can reach the site by taxis, buses, or private vehicles from Bhagalpur. Local facilities include basic amenities and guided tours provided by the Archaeological Survey of India, making exploration of the ruins convenient for tourists and pilgrims.
